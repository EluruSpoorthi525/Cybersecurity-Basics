# Security Controls

## Introduction

Security controls are safeguards or countermeasures implemented by an organization to reduce cybersecurity risks, protect assets, and ensure the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) of information.

Security controls help organizations prevent, detect, and respond to cyber threats while ensuring compliance with industry standards such as **ISO/IEC 27001**, **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)**, **NIST SP 800-53**, and **CIS Controls**.

Without security controls, organizations become vulnerable to cyberattacks, data breaches, insider threats, and system failures.

---

# What are Security Controls?

A **Security Control** is any administrative, technical, or physical measure implemented to reduce risk by protecting an organization's information systems and assets.

Security controls are designed to:

- Prevent security incidents
- Detect malicious activities
- Respond to attacks
- Recover from incidents
- Ensure regulatory compliance

---

# Objectives of Security Controls

Security controls aim to:

- Protect sensitive information
- Prevent unauthorized access
- Reduce cybersecurity risks
- Detect attacks quickly
- Maintain business continuity
- Comply with legal and regulatory requirements
- Protect organizational reputation

---

# Types of Security Controls

Security controls are commonly divided into three categories.

## 1. Administrative Controls

Administrative controls are policies, procedures, and management practices designed to reduce security risks.

These controls focus on people and processes rather than technology.

### Examples

- Information Security Policy
- Password Policy
- Acceptable Use Policy
- Security Awareness Training
- Incident Response Plan
- Risk Assessment
- Vendor Management
- Employee Background Verification

### Real-World Example

An organization requires every employee to complete annual cybersecurity awareness training to reduce phishing attacks.

---

## 2. Technical Controls

Technical controls use technology to protect systems and data.

They automatically enforce security measures.

### Examples

- Firewalls
- Antivirus Software
- Encryption
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
- Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS)
- Virtual Private Networks (VPN)
- Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
- Data Loss Prevention (DLP)
- Access Control Systems

### Real-World Example

A company enables Multi-Factor Authentication for all employee accounts to prevent unauthorized access even if passwords are stolen.

---

## 3. Physical Controls

Physical controls protect buildings, equipment, and other physical assets.

### Examples

- CCTV Cameras
- Security Guards
- Biometric Access
- Door Locks
- Smart Access Cards
- Alarm Systems
- Fire Suppression Systems
- Visitor Logs

### Real-World Example

Only authorized employees can enter the server room using fingerprint authentication.

---

# Functional Categories of Security Controls

Security controls can also be classified based on their purpose.

---

## Preventive Controls

Preventive controls stop security incidents before they happen.

### Examples

- Firewalls
- MFA
- Encryption
- Security Policies
- Access Control
- Security Awareness Training

---

## Detective Controls

Detective controls identify attacks after they occur.

### Examples

- Security Information and Event Management (SIEM)
- Log Monitoring
- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
- CCTV
- File Integrity Monitoring

---

## Corrective Controls

Corrective controls minimize damage after an incident.

### Examples

- Antivirus removing malware
- Restoring backups
- Patch Management
- Incident Response

---

## Deterrent Controls

Deterrent controls discourage attackers.

### Examples

- Warning Banners
- CCTV Cameras
- Security Guards
- Login Notifications

---

## Recovery Controls

Recovery controls restore systems after an incident.

### Examples

- Disaster Recovery Plans
- Data Backups
- Business Continuity Plans
- Cloud Recovery

---

## Compensating Controls

Compensating controls are alternative measures used when the primary control cannot be implemented.

### Example

If Multi-Factor Authentication cannot be enabled for a legacy application, the organization may restrict access through VPN and IP whitelisting.
